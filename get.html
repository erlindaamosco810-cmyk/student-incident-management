Help & Resources

The Problem What You Can Do
There has been a crime or someone is at immediate risk of harm. Emergency Hotline: Call 911 immediately.
Use this number if someone is in danger, there is a crime happening, or medical help is needed urgently. Do not wait—every second counts.
Additional steps:
1. Make sure you are safe before helping.
2. Provide clear details to the dispatcher (location, type of incident, number of people involved, and condition of anyone hurt).
3. If possible, stay on the line until help arrives.
Tip: Keep local police and barangay emergency contacts handy for faster response in your area.
Someone is feeling hopeless, helpless, or thinking of suicide. Call or text: 2919 (National Center for Mental Health Crisis Hotline)
Online chat support: NCMH Online Support
This service is free and confidential, available 24/7. Trained counselors listen, provide emotional support, and guide the person to professional help.
Signs to watch for: Talking about death, withdrawing from friends or family, sudden mood changes, or loss of interest in activities.
How to help:
1. Stay calm and listen carefully without judging.
2. Let them know they are not alone and that help is available.
3. Encourage professional help and, if necessary, stay with them until support arrives.
Reminder: Never ignore threats of self-harm. Acting early can save a life.
Someone is acting differently than normal, struggling to complete tasks, or unable to care for themselves. What to do:
1. Observe and take note of changes in behavior, mood, or routine. These could include withdrawal from friends/family, loss of interest in hobbies, confusion, or neglect of personal hygiene.
2. Encourage them to talk openly about how they feel without pressuring them.
3. Reach out to local mental health professionals, psychologists, or counselors.
4. Contact the Department of Health (DOH) for mental health programs and community support.
Early support can prevent bigger mental health issues. Small actions like listening, checking in regularly, and encouraging professional help make a big difference.
A child is being bullied in school. Steps to take:
1. Talk to the child calmly and listen to their experience. Let them know they are not alone.
2. Contact the teacher and explain the situation, including specific incidents and times.
3. Inform the school guidance counselor and principal. Provide any evidence if possible.
4. If unresolved, contact the Department of Education (DepEd) hotline: 1-800-1-DepEd (1-800-133733) or visit DepEd Resources.
Bullying affects mental health, confidence, and school performance. Early reporting helps protect the child and encourages a safer school environment.
Tip: Encourage the child to stay away from the bully when possible, build supportive friendships, and keep a record of bullying incidents.
A child is being cyberbullied. Steps to take:
1. Keep evidence of the bullying (screenshots, messages, emails, posts).
2. Report the incident to the platform (Facebook, TikTok, Instagram, Discord, etc.) using their “report abuse” feature.
3. Contact the National Center for Cybersecurity Incident Response (NCIRC) at ncirc.gov.ph for guidance.
4. Teach the child to block or mute the bully, avoid responding aggressively, and share experiences with a trusted adult.
Cyberbullying can impact mental health and self-esteem. Acting early, documenting incidents, and reporting to authorities are key steps.
Reminder: Never let the child face online bullying alone. Emotional support from family and professionals is essential.
School is not addressing harassment based on gender, religion, or disability. What to do:
1. Talk to the school principal or superintendent first and explain the incidents clearly.
2. If the school does not resolve the issue, contact the DepEd regional office for guidance.
3. For serious or ongoing harassment, contact the Commission on Human Rights (CHR) Philippines at 1343 or visit CHR Official Website.
CHR can investigate discrimination and harassment cases and ensure schools follow laws protecting students’ rights.
Tip: Keep detailed records of all incidents, communications with school officials, and any evidence. Documentation helps in filing formal complaints.
